# Coaching Research — How High School & Small College Football Coaches Actually Work

> Research compiled April 2026 from coaching publications, USA Football, Hudl, CoachFore, Coach & Athletic Director, vIQtory, FirstDown PlayBook, and direct coach interviews/forums.

---

## 1. WHO THESE COACHES ARE

### High School Head Coach Profile
- **Also a full-time teacher.** Teaches 5-6 classes per day. Coaching starts at 3:30pm after school.
- **Time commitment:** 30-35 hours/week on coaching ALONE, on top of full-time teaching (40hrs). Total: 65-75 hour weeks during season.
- **Staff:** 4-8 coaches total. Many are volunteers or part-time. Head coach often IS the offensive or defensive coordinator.
- **Budget:** $1,000-$3,000/year for tech. Some programs have zero tech budget — the coach pays out of pocket.
- **Film background:** Most coaches learned film study through experience, not training. Many are NOT tech-savvy. Some older coaches refuse to use computers for film work.
- **Pain point:** "I want to coach. Not be a data inputter or film guy." — direct quote from Hudl research.

### D2/D3 College Coach Profile
- **Head coach salary:** $43,000-70,000/year. Many D3 coaches also teach courses.
- **Staff:** 5 full-time coaches including head coach. Additional part-time assistants, grad assistants, student workers, and volunteers. Total staff ~7-10 including unpaid.
- **Multiple hats:** The head coach is also the OC or DC, the primary recruiter, the roster manager, the compliance officer, the budget manager, and sometimes the equipment coordinator.
- **No QC staff.** No analytics department. No dedicated film coordinator. One coach does everything the NFL has 15 people for.
- **Recruiting burden:** D2 coaches can contact recruits starting sophomore year. D3 has no formal contact restrictions but recruiting is constant and time-consuming. This time directly competes with game prep time.
- **Compliance:** Must handle NCAA compliance with no dedicated compliance staff in most cases. Rules change constantly (NIL, transfer portal, gambling). "Unclear roles and responsibilities leave staff unsure of who owns key compliance responsibilities."

## 2. THE WEEKLY COACHING CYCLE

### Saturday (Post-Game)
- Final breakdown of the game that was just played
- Players' grades on assignments (1 or 0 per play)
- Individual awards determined
- Film uploaded to Hudl by late Saturday/early Sunday
- This process should be complete by 4 PM Saturday

### Sunday
- **Morning:** Players come in, watch game film with position coaches, lift weights
- **Evening (7 PM):** Coaching staff begins initial breakdown of the upcoming opponent's most recent film
- Focus on: yard line, down and distance, series and hash
- Staff may watch 2-4 of the opponent's recent games
- **Goal:** Have the raw data organized by the end of Sunday night

### Monday
- **Staff arrives 7 AM** (before school starts for HS coaches)
- **OC task:** Detailed opponent analysis — fronts, pressures, coverage
- **DC task:** Opponent offense analysis — formations, run concepts, pass concepts
- **Film editing:** Staff uses Hudl to create cut-ups: all second-down passes, all 3rd-and-short plays, etc.
- **Scouting report production:** Staff creates reports from the opponent's prior 2 games
- **Play diagramming:** Coaches develop plays against the opponent's top alignments and stunts
- **Coverage pages:** OC produces coverage analysis for player film sessions
- **Monday evening:** Game plan put into paper and video format for players to see during Tuesday meetings

### Tuesday
- **Player meetings (before or after school):** Staff distributes and reviews the scouting report with players
- **Practice (2-2.5 hours):**
  - Individual period (15 min): each position works with position coach
  - Group period (15-20 min): offense and defense subgroups work together
  - Team period (30-40 min): 11-on-11
  - Focus: fundamentals, first and second down situations
  - Scout team runs opponent's base plays
  - 7-on-7 drills, screens and perimeter plays
- **Evening film session (30 min):** Practice keys + opponent base defenses

### Wednesday
- **Practice focus:** Third-down situations (short, medium, long)
- 3 scripted runs + 3 passes per situation vs scout team
- Second workout
- **Evening film session (30 min):** Opponent 3rd-down pressures and coverage
- By Wednesday, the game plan framework is largely set

### Thursday
- **Practice focus:** Situational offense — two-minute drill, red zone, goal line
- Green-zone (30-15 yard line) and red-zone (15-5) packages
- Goal-line and trick plays rehearsed
- Game plan finalized
- Call sheet and wristband card prepared
- **This is typically a lighter practice day** — walkthrough intensity, not full contact
- **Film session:** Final review of specific situations

### Friday (Game Day)
- **Morning (HS):** Final review of opening plays
- Mock game covering sudden-changes and special teams
- **For games that night:** Team meets in the afternoon for final walkthrough
- Game played

## 3. HOW COACHES BREAK DOWN FILM (THE MANUAL PROCESS)

### Step-by-step process most coaches follow:
1. **Watch the full game at regular speed first** — get a feel for the opponent's identity, tempo, philosophy
2. **Second watch: chart formations** — create a formation hit chart listing every formation used and how many times
3. **Third watch: chart by down and distance** — for each D&D situation, note formation, play type, direction, result
4. **Fourth watch: individual player evaluation** — grade specific players (their best, their worst, who to attack)
5. **Create tendency report** — compile the charted data into percentages: run/pass rate by D&D, formation frequency, favorite plays by situation

### What coaches specifically chart:
- **Formation frequency** — how often each formation appears
- **Run/pass ratio** by down and distance
- **Play direction** — do they favor one side?
- **Personnel groupings** — 11 (1RB 1TE), 12 (1RB 2TE), etc.
- **Motion and shift tendencies** — what happens after motion
- **Favorite plays by situation** — what they go to on 3rd and short, in the red zone, etc.
- **Individual player tells** — OL stance (light hands = pass), QB pre-snap routine
- **Blitz tendencies** — when, from where, who
- **Coverage tendencies** — by formation, by D&D

### Time investment:
- **4 games minimum** to get a good tendency sample across all situations
- **5-10 hours per opponent** for manual charting (the weekend killer)
- **Most of this time is data entry, not analysis** — the insight comes in the last hour, but the data collection takes the first 9 hours

### Tools used:
- **Hudl** — primary film platform for 90%+ of HS and small college programs
- **Hudl Assist** — auto-tags basics (formation, D&D, play type), saves some charting time
- **Spreadsheets** — many coaches use Excel/Google Sheets for tendency charts
- **Whiteboards** — physically drawing formations and tendencies for teaching
- **Pen and paper** — many coaches still chart by hand on scouting forms
- **Custom Hudl columns** — advanced coaches add custom breakdown columns for deeper analysis

## 4. THE SCOUTING REPORT (WHAT COACHES ACTUALLY PRODUCE)

### A complete scouting report includes:
1. **Team overview** — record, conference, offensive/defensive identity
2. **Offensive formation chart** — every formation with play count
3. **Formation-to-play tendency** — what they run from each formation
4. **Down and distance breakdown** — play selection by situation
5. **Run game analysis** — primary concepts, blocking schemes, key tells
6. **Pass game analysis** — favorite routes/concepts, QB tendencies (launch chart showing targets, throwing locations)
7. **Red zone analysis** — what changes inside the 20
8. **Key player profiles** — individual grades on best/worst players
9. **Defensive breakdown** (if scouting their defense):
   - Front alignment by formation
   - Coverage by D&D and formation
   - Blitz packages with diagrams
   - Stunts
10. **"Tells"** — pre-snap indicators: light stances (pass), staring at assignment, large splits (pass), guard leaning forward (pull play)
11. **Personnel information** — starters, numbers, class, height, weight

### How the scouting report is used:
- Given to players in position meetings
- Projected on screen during film sessions
- Printed as handouts for players to study
- Informs the game plan on The Board
- Drives practice plan and scout team card design

## 5. PRACTICE STRUCTURE IN DETAIL

### Standard 2-hour practice format:

```
TIME    PERIOD              DESCRIPTION
0:00    Stretch/warm-up     Dynamic stretching, form running
0:10    Special teams       5-10 minutes, rotate daily (kickoff Mon, punt Tue, etc.)
0:20    Individual (INDY)   Position-specific drills with position coach
0:35    Group period        Subgroups: OL+RB work run fits, QB+WR throw routes,
                            DL+LB work stunts, DB work coverage
0:55    Team period         11v11 vs scout team. Run through game plan situations.
1:25    Situational         Specific situations: 2-minute drill, red zone, 3rd down
1:45    Conditioning        Sprints, gassers
1:55    Team break          
2:00    END
```

### How practice changes through the week:
- **Monday:** Heavy installation. New game plan concepts taught. Full pads. Most physical.
- **Tuesday:** Refine installations. Full or shoulder pads. Scout team runs opponent's top 8-10 plays.
- **Wednesday:** Third-down focus. Competitive team periods. Medium intensity.
- **Thursday:** Walkthrough speed. Review. Situational. No full contact. Mental prep.

### Scout team operation:
- **Scout team = backup players** who run the opponent's plays against the starters
- **Scout team cards are CRITICAL.** Each card shows:
  - Formation diagram (in color, with jersey numbers)
  - Motion/shift if applicable
  - Play action (the concept being run)
  - Coach notes on emphasis
- Cards must be "done neatly and with great detail... in color with jersey numbers or player initials"
- The scout team coordinator manages the look — their job is to make the scout team LOOK like the opponent
- "Their number one job is to look like the team you are about to play"
- At HS level, scout team players often don't fully understand what they're running — the cards are essential

## 6. FILM SESSIONS WITH PLAYERS

### How coaches teach through film:
- **Classroom setting:** Projector in a room, entire position group watches together
- **Coach controls playback:** Play, pause, rewind, frame advance
- **Coach draws on screen:** Circle players, draw arrows, indicate assignments
- **Interactive teaching:** Coach asks "what do you see?" before showing the answer
- **Player notebooks:** Players write notes, expected to reference them later
- **Individual assignments:** "On clip 53, you missed your block — go watch that tonight"

### What coaches show:
1. **Previous game film (own team):** What went right, what didn't. Individual grades.
2. **Opponent film:** Formation recognition, what to look for pre-snap, their best plays
3. **Specific matchups:** Your position vs the opponent's position. "Watch #72. When he's light on his hand, it's a pass."

### Player film study expectations:
- Position groups watch opponent film for ~2 hours/week
- Some programs require written reports: "Players write one to two paragraphs on what they saw"
- Players present to the group: "Different players get up in front of the group to read their reports, show clips, and answer questions"
- Players review on their own via Hudl on their phones

## 7. THE CALL SHEET AND WRISTBAND

### Offensive call sheet structure:
- **One laminated page** the OC holds on the sideline
- **Front side:**
  - Opening script (first 10-15 plays)
  - Plays organized by situation: 1st down, 2nd and short, 2nd and long, 3rd and short, 3rd and medium, 3rd and long
  - Red zone plays
  - Two-minute offense plays
  - Goal line
  - Trick plays / specials
  - Pregame schedule notes
  - Officials' names
  - Timeout counter
- **Back side:**
  - Personnel grouping quick reference
  - Audible options
  - Formation adjustments
  - Notes from the scouting report (quick reminders)
- The OC has **less than 30 seconds between plays** to make a call. The sheet must be organized for speed.

### QB wristband card:
- **Physical card that inserts into a wrist sleeve**
- Typically 20-40 plays
- Plays organized by number code: coach calls "23" from the sideline, QB looks at wristband, finds play 23
- **Color-coded by situation category** for fast visual reference
- Multiple wristbands may be used: one per series of games, adjusted weekly
- Format: small grid with play number → play name/formation in abbreviated team terminology

### Opening script:
- **First 10-15 plays of the game, pre-determined**
- Invented by Bill Walsh (49ers). Now used at every level.
- **Purpose:**
  1. See how the defense adjusts to different formations/motions
  2. Get the QB in rhythm with easy throws
  3. Attack known weaknesses early
  4. Show plays that will be built on later (set up play-action)
  5. Get the best player the ball
- **The script is NOT locked in** — coaches adjust based on what happens. If play 3 shows a defensive weakness, they may skip to a play designed to exploit it.

## 8. PLAYER GRADING SYSTEM

### The 1 or 0 system (most common at HS level):
- Each player gets a **1 (did their job) or 0 (didn't)** on every play
- No bonus points, no deductions, no complexity
- **Target: 80% individual, 75% team average**
- Position coach grades 3-4 players per play (their position group)
- Grading is done after the game (Saturday/Sunday) watching film
- Grade sheets distributed to players

### Alternative: Plus/Minus system
- Each player gets a + or - per play
- Total pluses ÷ total plays = grade percentage
- Some coaches add categories: assignment, effort, technique

### How grades are used:
- **Depth chart decisions** — grade directly affects playing time
- **Individual development** — "the hawk's eye for issues causing zeros"
- **Player accountability** — grades are public within the position group
- **Awards** — weekly awards for highest-graded players
- **Uploaded to Hudl** with notes so players can review their specific plays

## 9. HALFTIME ADJUSTMENTS

### The 15-minute halftime structure:
- **First 5 minutes:** Staff meets WITHOUT players. Coordinators review:
  - Offensive: what fronts they're seeing by D&D, coverage by D&D/formation, play selection success/failure
  - Defensive: formation tendencies emerging, who to blitz, coverage adjustments
- **Next 10 minutes:** Teach the adjustments to players by position group
  - "Great programs meet 5 and teach 10. Average programs meet 10 and teach 5."
- **Key principle:** "Change for change's sake is a bad idea." Only adjust what's specifically not working.
- **Audible's opportunity:** If Audible could process first-half film during halftime and surface "here's what changed" — that would be transformative. Even just organizing the first-half data by D&D would save the staff 3 of their 5 meeting minutes.

## 10. SELF-SCOUTING

### Why coaches self-scout:
- "Finding our tendencies is the primary reason most coaches self scout"
- "We have to make sure that a certain formation isn't always leading to the same play — aka tipping our pitches"
- Good opponents WILL chart your tendencies and exploit them

### How coaches self-scout:
1. Sort all plays by formation family, then by play direction
2. Look for formations that always lead to the same play
3. Check personnel group tendencies: "Does 12 personnel always mean run?"
4. Check motion tendencies: "Does jet motion always mean sweep?"
5. Review down and distance patterns: "Do we always throw on 3rd and long?"

### Time investment:
- 30-40 minutes reviewing data
- 15-20 minutes discussing as a staff
- "Don't let it consume your weekends"
- Usually done after every 3-4 games, not weekly

### Breaking tendencies:
- Identify the most predictable patterns
- Add wrinkles: flea-flicker off a formation that always shows run
- Use formations deceptively: show a look, do something different
- Some teams intentionally create false tendency data for future opponents to study

## 11. TECHNOLOGY PAIN POINTS (WHAT COACHES ACTUALLY SAY)

### Direct quotes and insights from coaches:

> "Rather than the first four hours of your game planning week gathering data, gathering film, organizing — I want to start my game planning with those things ready."

> "I want video game style replay where I can show a play from a player's perspective and teach them from that... for all 22 players."

> "Being able to focus on coaching more than being a data inputter or film guy. I want to coach."

> "I want all the stuff that I want and want it to be there. Video from every angle, all the data."

### Top frustrations:
1. **Time spent on data entry, not analysis** — the charting takes 8 hours, the thinking takes 1
2. **Toggling between multiple tools** — Hudl for film, spreadsheet for tendencies, diagramming app for plays, depth chart somewhere else
3. **"Programs running 6+ disconnected tools report higher frustration and lower adoption"**
4. **Tech literacy gaps** — older coaches won't use the computer; younger coaches can but it's still slow
5. **Film without context** — pushing clips to players without teaching them what to look for
6. **Drawing tools are essential** — "Drawing lines, circles, and arrows directly onto the video is the clearest way to illustrate concepts"
7. **No integrated platform** — "An integrated platform will outperform three specialized tools — even if each specialized tool is better at its specific function"

### What coaches wish existed:
- Auto-generated scouting reports from film
- Player-perspective replay for teaching
- Automated tendency charting (the 8-hour job done in seconds)
- Practice plans that connect to the game plan
- One platform for everything (film, game plan, communication, grading)
- Visual annotations on film for teaching players

## 12. THE COACHING STAFF INTERACTION MODEL

### How the staff works together:

**Head Coach:**
- Sets overall game strategy
- May be OC or DC
- Makes final decisions on game plan
- Manages staff assignments
- At HS: often does everything because staff is limited

**Offensive Coordinator:**
- Breaks down opponent defense (fronts, pressures, coverage)
- Builds the offensive game plan
- Creates the call sheet
- Calls plays on game day (or head coach does)
- Assigns: OL coach → run game, WR coach → pass concepts, RB coach → screens

**Defensive Coordinator:**
- Breaks down opponent offense (formations, concepts, tendencies)
- Builds the defensive game plan (fronts, coverages, blitzes)
- Calls defensive adjustments on game day

**Position Coaches:**
- Grade their position group players (1 or 0 per play)
- Run individual period drills
- Prepare scout team cards for their side of the ball
- Teach film to their position group
- Upload practice film notes to Hudl each night

### At smaller programs (HS, D3):
- Head coach IS the OC or DC
- One coach may handle 2-3 position groups
- Volunteers may handle a position group but need detailed guidance
- "The head coach has a much more complete hold on the intricacies of the team"
- GAs and student assistants handle film uploading, equipment, logistics

## 13. IMPLICATIONS FOR AUDIBLE

### What Audible must replace or enhance:
1. **The 8-hour charting session** → auto-generated tendency data from Hudl import
2. **The scouting report creation** → AI-generated, football-specific, structured report
3. **The call sheet building** → game plan builder with tendency-backed suggestions
4. **The scout team card creation** → auto-generated from opponent playbook extraction
5. **The wristband card** → auto-formatted PDF from the game plan
6. **The film session prep** → curated clips with teaching context + visual annotations
7. **The player grading process** → (future) auto-grading from CV analysis

### What Audible must NOT try to replace:
1. **The coaching** — the actual teaching, motivation, and relationship-building
2. **The play calling** — the OC decides what to call, not the AI
3. **The practice** — physical reps on the field can't be digitized
4. **The halftime talk** — the human element of adjustments
5. **The depth chart decisions** — coaches decide who plays

### Key design implications:
- **Must work between classes** — coaches have 20-minute windows, not hour-long sessions
- **Must work on iPad at practice** — not just desktop
- **Must integrate, not fragment** — one tool, not six
- **Must speak football** — formations, not "categories." Snaps, not "data points."
- **Must show film, not charts** — coaches trust their eyes more than numbers
- **Must be simple enough for volunteer coaches** — if only the HC can use it, it fails
- **Drawing tools are non-negotiable** — coaches MUST draw on film
- **Scout team cards must be printable** — they're used on the practice field, not on a screen
